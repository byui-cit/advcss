WDD 331 Course Introduction

Description

This course is designed in a way that tries to recognize that most of the specific technologies we could study related to CSS will change. The placement of the first outcome is not random.

It is the most important thing the students could gain from this course.

This course is also repeatable. The course design is also to accommodate that. The first four weeks are set, they will always be the same thing:

  1. Review and introduction
  2. Selectors
  3. SCSS
  4. Zen Garden introduction

Also during those first four weeks the students will be asked to pay close attention to every website they visit. They should take note of anything that catches their attention. They are looking for things that the builder of the site has done that they think are cool and that they would be interested in learning how to replicate. The ideas can be submitted at anytime during the first 4 weeks to a shared Trello board, but each student must submit 3-4 total by the end of week 4. They will submit the links in Ilearn at that time as well to aid the instructor in grading this participation.

During weeks 5-12 of the semester items will be chosen from the list, assigned a complexity and time, and the class will learn to re-create the example. When one is done another will be chosen. These can take anywhere from a few days to up to three weeks.

Spend a few minutes each week highlighting some of the ideas to build excitement and remind the students they are supposed to be paying attention to the sites they visit.

Working the examples

You should plan on holding a weekly office hour or lab. Try and find a time that works for as many students as possible, then record and post it for those that can't make it. You can use this time to respond to questions on ongoing examples, give a post-mortem on a finished example, or to kick off a new example.

As you start a new example begin by helping the students to deconstruct the problem. Ideally this will result in the students having a list of technologies that they will need to study in order to solve the problem, and a rough blueprint for how to proceed. The instructor can give them some resources to help with this, but the students should be encouraged to find their own as well and to share what they learn with the rest of the class.

You can use this: Collection of learning modules (work in progress) as a starting point for resources.

Repeating students

For students taking the course for a second time, use them to help curate the ideas being submitted during the first four weeks. They can help you prioritize the list, and get an initial estimate of complexity. They are welcome to submit their own examples as well. You could even get them started on solving one of the examples early if they need more to do. You could have them share their solution with the rest of the class as an introduction to week5. If face-to-face they can take a few minutes in class, if online ask them to prepare a short video talking about the process they followed and what they learned that can be shared with the class.

After week 4 the course will run the same for repeating students as for the other students.

Collaboration

You have the option of collaborating with the other WDD 331 instructors to reduce the amount of time spent in preparation. One way this could look is that in week 4 the instructors that would like to collaborate would meet and discuss the submitted examples. Together all the involved instructors would come up with a curated list. All the sections would then work through the same list. This would allow the sharing of resources and ideas between sections.

The Course Lead would always be willing to participate in the collaboration, if other instructors are interested.

Best Practices

Our goal is not to just solve the problems, but solve them using best practices as well. As you select examples to work on watch for opportunities to insert things from the following list as additional requirements as appropriate:

Course Setup

You will need to create a Microsoft Team for your section, and add all of your students. If you would prefer Slack that is also fine. Communicate to the class which platform you will be using. You will also need to create a Trello board and add the students to that as well. Post the link to the board in the messaging system you have chosen.

You can use this board as a starting point. That link will invite you to the board...then you can go into settings and copy it you your Trello account. You can either send an invite to each student to join your board (copy/paste the list of emails into the invite box), or enable the invite link and send it to the class.

Student Feedback

Students can receive feedback in one of two ways. First they can ask in the weekly reflection that they will be completing each week. Second they can ask the instructor at anytime for specific feedback on something they are working on. Encourage students to take advantage of this. Because the final grade is not directly tied to the assignments they are working on (see below) it frees the instructor to offer as much help as needed. Students have a bit of trouble adapting to this so encourage them to come with questions before the due dates!

Most of the assignments are formative...their purpose is to help the student learn and not to assess what they know. See below for how the assessment will take place.

Grading

Each week the students will be required to complete a reflection on what they learned that week, along with any questions or requests for feedback from the instructor. These will make up 30% of the student's final grade. The other 70% of the grade will come from a self-evaluation of themselves on the course Outcomes. This can be done in one of two ways.

  1. The student can schedule a meeting with the instructor for a synchronous (face-to-face or online) meeting during the last week of the semester. This is done as an interview where the student offers a claim of mastery, and the instructor challenges as needed. The student should answer the challenges by showing examples of their work that they feel justify their claim.
  2. The student can prepare a short video of themselves giving their self-evaluation along with examples of the work they have completed over the semester that back up their claims. The links to the work should be submitted with the review as a portfolio.

I would recommend picking one of these methods and have all students do it that way for consistency. Here are two resources that can help you and the students prepare for this:

  1. Course Outcomes
  2. Good warmup exercise for the interview. You might ask the student to review this Codepen and respond to the following prompt: "What process would you follow if you were trying to figure out how to re-create the example?" I will often have them give their answer and then say: 'let's do it!' and dig into it together for a few minutes. If done during a synchronous interview you can usually tell exactly where a student is in relation to the outcomes after this discussion.

Warning!

Because the assignments are not graded for points, they do not immediately affect the student's grade. We have removed the 'big stick'. Sometimes that results in the students lowering the priority on time spent in this class. Warn them about doing this. If you sense a problem with priority, you could always add an assignment in the LMS and have the students turn in the examples they work up. This would be added when you negotiate with the class which example will be done next, and how much time will be given to it so that you know when to set the due date for.

I would recommend that you treat these submissions as mostly pass/fail. The course design attempts to free up as much of the instructor time as possible to advise and guide, and as little as possible grading. Try not to lose too much of your time in grading these submissions if you choose to add them. Follow the pattern of letting the student request feedback as needed, and let the assignment submission 'due date' provide a bit of extra incentive to get the work done.
